# Semiotic foundations of Codex Signum: a critical validation

**Codex Signum rests on largely sound semiotic theory, but harbors three critical flaws: a corrected but instructive misclassification of spatial proximity, a tension between continuous encoding and Saussurean discreteness, and a dangerously small primitive set of just 6 morphemes.** The system's application of Peircean sign classification, Eco's overcoding/undercoding framework, and Barthes' orders of signification is theoretically rigorous and well-aligned with established scholarship. However, claims of cultural universality for visual encodings are only partially supported — pulsation and spatial proximity are well-grounded in innate perceptual mechanisms, luminance is moderately grounded, and color mappings are definitively not universal. The "Halo aesthetic" functions precisely as Barthesian myth but encodes Western techno-utopian values that will not transfer globally. AI agents can achieve functional semiotic competence but not genuine semiotic understanding in Harnad's grounded sense.

This report validates or challenges each of the system's theoretical claims against Peirce, Saussure, Eco, Barthes, Kress & van Leeuwen, and Groupe μ, drawing on cross-cultural empirical research, historical constructed language analysis, and computational semiotics literature.

---

## 1. Peircean foundations are strong but demand hybrid classification

Codex Signum's application of Peirce's triadic sign relation — representamen, object, interpretant — is theoretically sound, with one critical correction already identified and validated by this review.

**Graph topology as diagrammatic iconicity is correct.** Peirce's icon subtypes (image, diagram, metaphor) map cleanly onto the system's encoding strategy. For abstract concepts like governance, resonance, and maturity, imagistic iconicity is indeed impossible — these concepts have no direct visual appearance. The system correctly relies on **diagrammatic iconicity** (structural parallels between sign and object) and **metaphoric iconicity** (cross-domain mappings). This classification is well-supported by Stjernfelt's *Diagrammatology* (2007), which argues diagrams are Peirce's most important icon subtype because "by the direct observation of [diagrams] other truths concerning [their] object can be discovered than those which suffice to determine [their] construction" (CP 2.279). Nöth (2023) cautions against reducing all iconicity to the diagrammatic — the metaphorical component (luminance→health as a cross-domain mapping from light/vitality to system wellness) is equally important and should be explicitly acknowledged.

**The spatial proximity correction is validated and consequential.** The original claim that "spatial proximity is indexical" was correctly identified as an error. Indexes require **causal or existential connection** — smoke indexes fire because combustion *causes* smoke; a weathervane indexes wind because wind *physically compels* its direction. Spatial proximity in a graph visualization is a **design decision** mapping semantic relatedness to visual closeness — a structural parallel, not a causal trace. This makes it diagrammatic iconicity, not indexicality. The distinction matters operationally: indexes are processed more automatically (the causal connection is often self-evident), while diagrams require recognition of structural similarity, demanding explicit training. The London Underground map, analyzed by Atã, Bitarello, and Queiroz (2014) in *Cognitive Semiotics*, is the paradigmatic case — it preserves topological relations without geographic resemblance, functioning through diagrammatic rather than indexical logic.

**Graph edges present a genuine hybrid case.** An edge between Service A and Service B exists *because* A calls B — there is an existential connection, lending indexical character. But the edge is also a representational artifact rendered by a designer's algorithm, giving it diagrammatic and symbolic properties. Peirce himself recognized by 1903 that pure instances of any single sign type are nearly impossible. Codex Signum should explicitly embrace this hybridity: edges are **primarily diagrammatic icons with significant indexical and symbolic components**. This mixed classification aligns with contemporary Peircean scholarship (Keane 2003, Thellefsen & Friedman 2023).

**Luminance and color classifications require nuance.** Luminance=health is not purely symbolic (arbitrary convention) nor purely iconic. It exploits the cross-cultural metaphor GOOD IS LIGHT (Lakoff & Johnson), making it a **motivated symbol with metaphoric iconic grounding**. The specific mapping (0.8 = "healthy") is conventional and must be learned, but the direction (brighter = better) leverages pre-existing associations. Color=domain/type, by contrast, is **purely symbolic** — no inherent connection exists between a wavelength and a software category.

## 2. Saussurean structuralism applies with a critical caveat about continuity

The system's use of differential value and paradigmatic opposition is textbook-accurate in principle but encounters a genuine theoretical tension with continuous encoding.

**Paradigmatic contrasts are validly applied.** Defining a "Growth" glyph by opposition to "decay," "stasis," and "collapse" is a correct application of Saussurean differential value. Meaning emerges precisely from what a sign *excludes* — "in language, there are only differences without positive terms" (Saussure 1916). The paradigmatic set {growth, decay, stasis, collapse} forms a well-structured opposition where each term derives meaning from contrast with the others. Kress and van Leeuwen (1996) explicitly extend paradigmatic analysis to visual systems, arguing that visual communication operates through systematic choices analogous to linguistic paradigmatic relations, though culturally situated rather than universal.

**Continuous luminance encoding creates a genuine Saussurean problem.** Saussure's *langue* requires **discrete distinctions** — phonemes are categorically distinct (/p/ vs. /b/), with no meaningful intermediate. A luminance gradient from 0.0 to 1.0 is an analogical, continuous encoding that violates this requirement. As Chandler notes, "signifying systems impose digital order on what we often experience as a dynamic and seamless flux. The very definition of something as a sign involves reducing the continuous to the discrete." Saussure himself used the color spectrum as an example: frequencies are meaningless until language carves them into discrete categories like "blue" and "green."

The resolution lies in recognizing that **Saussure's framework handles the categorical thresholds while Peirce's handles the continuous signal**. For Saussurean validity, the system should discretize luminance into categorical states — "healthy" (>0.7), "warning" (0.4–0.7), "critical" (<0.4) — creating proper paradigmatic oppositions. The continuous gradient can function simultaneously as a Peircean icon (qualitative similarity varying by degree). This dual-framework approach is not contradictory but complementary. Perceptual science (Ware, *Information Visualization*) confirms that humans can reliably discriminate only **3–4 luminance levels** simultaneously, suggesting natural discretization occurs perceptually regardless of encoding continuity.

**Saussure's framework has limited but productive applicability to visual systems.** Critics correctly note that Saussure focused exclusively on linguistic signs, emphasized linearity (temporal ordering), and insisted on arbitrariness — all features that conflict with visual signs, which are spatial, multidimensional, and often motivated. However, his deeper structural insights about differential meaning, systematic opposition, and synchronic analysis have been productively extended to visual semiotics by Barthes, Kress and van Leeuwen, and Eco. The extension requires acknowledging that visual paradigmatic systems are less rigid than linguistic ones and that visual signs are frequently motivated rather than arbitrary.

## 3. Eco's framework strongly validates dual-mode encoding

Eco's theory of codes, overcoding/undercoding, and open/closed texts provides the most robust theoretical support for Codex Signum's architecture.

**The overcoded/undercoded duality is not merely compatible with Eco — it is a central Eco insight.** In *A Theory of Semiotics* (1976) and *The Role of the Reader* (1979), Eco demonstrated that overcoding and undercoding "go on at the same time" and are "often difficult to judge between." Overcoding adds rules to constrain interpretation (luminance 0.8 = health score 0.8 is a textbook case of an explicit correlation rule layered on a basic encoding). Undercoding involves provisional, tentative interpretation of units without established rules (an Astral Script glyph for "governance" requiring contextual interpretation). The claim that the same structural encoding can support both modes is **explicitly validated** by Eco's own framework — this is not a tension to resolve but a feature of how all semiotic systems actually operate.

**The open/closed text distinction supports context-dependent interpretation.** Eco moved from rigid categorization to a spectrum model where texts have varying degrees of closure. Pezzini (2015, *Semiotica*) developed the insight that "nothing is more open than a closed text" — even highly constrained texts contain dimensions requiring reader cooperation. The system's claim that operational monitoring functions as a closed text (constrained interpretation, programmed readings) while Human-AI communication functions as an open text (multiple valid readings, encyclopedic competence required) is well-supported. The same system operating in both modes depending on context is precisely what Eco's framework predicts.

**Eco's encyclopedia model elegantly accommodates multi-layered meaning.** Against the dictionary model (fixed, hierarchical definitions), Eco argued for the encyclopedia: networked, rhizomatic, open-ended — "a vast, multidimensional net of associations." Codex Signum's multiple encoding channels (topology, luminance, color, pulsation, spatial relationships) create interconnected pathways to meaning where no single layer provides a complete definition. However, this creates an acknowledged tension: overcoded operational mappings function like dictionary entries within what is structurally an encyclopedic system. Eco himself recognized this as a practical necessity — "local dictionaries" serving pragmatic purposes within the encyclopedic whole.

One important caveat: **Eco would insist that even overcoded readings are conventional, not natural.** The apparent objectivity of "luminance 0.8 = health 0.8" is itself a form of naturalization — it renders invisible the assumptions embedded in quantifying health on a 0-1 scale and mapping it to brightness. This connects directly to Barthes' mythology (see below).

## 4. The Halo aesthetic is valid Barthesian myth but culturally bound

Barthes' orders of signification — denotation, connotation, mythology — map precisely onto Codex Signum's layered meaning architecture. The application is textbook-accurate. But the mythological layer reveals a critical vulnerability.

**The three-layer mapping is rigorous.** Denotation (luminance 0.8 = health metric of 0.8), connotation (bright = good/active, dim = bad/inactive), and myth (the Halo aesthetic = advanced AI civilization, Human-AI symbiosis as natural/inevitable) correctly instantiate Barthes' framework from *Mythologies* (1957). Barthes' key insight — that myth's function is to **"transform history into nature,"** making culturally constructed meanings appear eternal and innocent — applies with particular force here. The aesthetic conventions of clean lines, light/energy, and ethereal balance are presented as naturally evoking sophistication, but they are historically specific artifacts of Western design lineage: Bauhaus functionalism, Dieter Rams, Apple design language, and Silicon Valley techno-utopianism, filtered through sci-fi visual culture (Tron, Ghost in the Shell, Mass Effect).

**The Halo aesthetic encodes Western techno-utopian values, not universal ones.** Barthes' analysis of the Citroën DS in *Mythologies* is directly analogous — he described the car as "an object fallen from the sky," where seamless curves mythologized mobility as effortless transcendence while masking industrial exploitation. The Halo aesthetic similarly mythologizes Human-AI interaction as "effortless symbiosis," potentially masking power asymmetries and labor displacement. East Asian design traditions like Japanese *wabi-sabi* value imperfection, asymmetry, and natural patina — values antithetical to the Halo aesthetic. The system's designers must decide whether this mythological dimension is intentional (deliberately constructing a shared visual vocabulary) or problematic (imposing culturally specific values as universal).

**Color and form associations are partially universal, partially contingent.** Jonauskaite et al. (2020, *Psychological Science*, 30 nations, 4,598 participants) found universal color-emotion association patterns (average similarity r = .88) but with significant local differences — Chinese participants associated joy with red; Nigerian participants associated fear with red. Adams and Osgood's 23-culture study (1973) found red is perceived as "active" and "strong" cross-culturally, while black is "bad" and white is "good" — but the specific valence varies (white = mourning in parts of East Asia, red = danger in Western but prosperity in Chinese contexts). For form, angular shapes triggering threat perception has ecological basis (thorns, fangs), and curves connoting safety appears broadly cross-cultural, but the specific symbolic meanings (circles = harmony, triangles = dynamism) are substantially culturally modulated. A 2025 UC Berkeley study comparing UK/US with rural Namibian populations found "profound differences in visual processing, including aspects previously assumed to be universal."

## 5. Dynamic graph visualizations require new visual grammar beyond Kress and van Leeuwen

The compositional frameworks of Kress and van Leeuwen and Groupe μ provide essential theoretical foundations but were designed for static images and require significant extension for dynamic, interactive media.

**Kress and van Leeuwen's spatial semantics become unstable in dynamic visualizations.** The Given/New (left/right), Ideal/Real (top/bottom), and Center/Margin compositional principles assume a fixed, bounded visual field. In graph visualizations where elements move, zoom, and reposition, these spatial semantics dissolve. Moreover, the Given/New distinction is explicitly Western-layout-specific — it reverses for Arabic and Hebrew readers. A 2025 study in *ACM Transactions on Applied Perception* found that RTL readers show elevated confusion when temporal graphs flow left-to-right, directly challenging the universality of horizontal information value.

**Dynamic media generate new semiotic resources.** Recent animation semiotics research (Punctum 10:1, 2024) identifies several: **metamorphic signification** (visible transformation as meaning-making), **temporal sequencing** (order of appearance encodes narrative structure), **motion as vector** (replacing static representational vectors), and **interactivity** (user-controlled reading paths that override compositional grammar). For Codex Signum, this means the system must establish **temporal grammar** alongside spatial grammar — what appears first establishes "given" information; what appears later is "new." Motion direction, speed, and acceleration become primary meaning-bearing variables.

**Groupe μ's plastic/iconic distinction is highly relevant.** Codex Signum primarily uses **plastic signs** — non-representational visual variables (color, luminance, shape, spatial position) that generate meaning through systematic contrast and opposition rather than resemblance. Groupe μ (1992) theorized that plastic signs can generate meaning autonomously, not merely as connotative adjuncts to iconic content. But meaning in purely plastic sign systems relies on **learned codes** — viewers must learn that a circle means X while a triangle means Y. There is no shortcut through resemblance. This has direct implications for training requirements and onboarding, and aligns with the finding that spatial proximity functions as diagrammatic iconicity (requiring learned structural mapping) rather than indexicality (requiring no training).

## 6. Six morphemes is almost certainly insufficient — the oligosynthesis problem

The system's use of modular composition from geometric primitives follows a proven strategy, but the claim that 6 morphemes suffice faces serious challenges from both linguistic theory and historical precedent.

**No successful encoding system operates with so few primitives.** Musical notation uses approximately 20 core symbols and covers a constrained domain. Chinese characters rely on 214 Kangxi radicals (functionally ~500+ components) for a full writing system. Blissymbolics used 100+ base symbols and still failed for general communication. Mathematical notation employs roughly 50 core symbols. aUI attempted 42 primitives and experienced total adoption failure. The IPA uses 107+ symbols for phonetic transcription alone. **Six primitives places Codex Signum in the territory of oligosynthetic languages, which linguists consider "impossible or impractical for productive use by humans"** (no natural language exemplifies this category).

The combinatorial mathematics is straightforward: 6 binary-present primitives yield only 64 combinations; ordered pairs give 36 bigrams; trigrams give 216. Even with spatial composition and multiple visual channels, the expressive space is severely limited compared to the semantic domain the system claims to cover. The fundamental risk is the **decomposition trap** demonstrated by aUI: when everything must be composed from minimal primitives, even simple concepts require complex multi-element chains. aUI's word for "cat" was *bôzvos* ("Together-Five-Part-Make-animal") — five morphemes chained to express a single basic concept. Users abandon such systems when the effort-to-benefit ratio exceeds natural language alternatives.

**However, 6 morphemes could work under specific conditions.** If the morphemes function not as discrete semantic units (like aUI's elements) but as **dimensions of a multi-dimensional visual encoding space** (shape channel + color channel + luminance channel + position channel + size channel + motion channel), they define a continuous multi-dimensional space whose combinatorial richness far exceeds sequential composition. If the system is genuinely **domain-constrained** (Human-AI communication about system state, not general communication), the semantic space may be tractable. And if additional composition rules, spatial grammars, and contextual modifiers supplement the 6 base morphemes, effective expressiveness can be significantly expanded.

The empirical sweet spot for domain-specific visual languages appears to be **15–50 core primitives** (road signs ~50, musical notation ~20). For Codex Signum's stated domain, **expanding to 12–20 primitives** would significantly reduce compound complexity while maintaining learnability. The design should follow Chinese characters' dual-coding strategy: combine semantic morphemes with structural/positional cues to multiply expressiveness without proportionally increasing the primitive inventory.

## 7. Multi-layered interpretation clusters at 3 functional layers, not 2

The claim that "effective multi-layered systems are 2-3 layers deep" is a useful practical heuristic but oversimplifies the empirical evidence.

**Successful systems consistently exhibit 3–4 structural layers.** Musical notation operates across 4 layers: note reading (1–2 years), dynamics and phrasing (3–5 years), harmonic analysis (5–10 years), and historical performance conventions (10+ years). Chinese characters have 3+ layers: whole-character recognition, radical decomposition, and etymological knowledge. The WIPP nuclear waste marker system explicitly designs for 4–5 levels of progressive disclosure, from instinctive danger recognition through full technical documentation. Chemical formulas operate at 3 layers: composition, bonding structure, and 3D conformation.

**The "2-3 layers" claim is best understood as a practical usability ceiling, not an absolute constraint.** Each layer roughly doubles in acquisition time. Layer 1 is accessible to most users (basic literacy). Layer 2 is accessible to trained practitioners. Layers 3+ are accessible only to specialists. Progressive disclosure research from Nielsen Norman Group and industry practice suggests that "more than 2 levels of information disclosure usually negatively affect user experience" for general interfaces. But specialist systems with explicitly segmented audiences can justify more layers.

**Cognitive load theory provides the binding constraint.** Sweller's framework identifies working memory capacity (~4 chunks in contemporary research) as the hard limit on simultaneous processing. Each additional encoding channel adds at minimum one element of interactivity. With 5+ simultaneous visual encodings (luminance, pulsation, color, size, position), the system risks exceeding working memory limits for novice users who lack schemas for automatic processing. The **expertise reversal effect** (Kalyuga et al. 2003) compounds this: scaffolding designed for novices (redundant encoding, explicit labels) actively harms expert performance. A single fixed multi-layered display cannot optimally serve both populations. The solution is adaptive display with distinct novice and expert modes, following Shneiderman's mantra: "overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand."

## 8. Grounding varies from strong (pulsation) to weak (color semantics)

The symbol grounding problem — how signs connect to what they represent — reveals a gradient of grounding strength across Codex Signum's encodings, with important implications for both human and AI interpreters.

**Pulsation is the most strongly grounded encoding.** The perception of rhythmic motion as indicating life and activity is hardwired in vertebrate perceptual systems. Johansson (1973) demonstrated that humans perceive biological motion from just a few moving points on body joints. Troje and Westhoff (2006) identified an innate "life detector" present in newly hatched chicks — requiring no prior learning. Scholl and Tremoulet (2000) showed that "causal relations and animacy are rich and vivid properties of visual displays" that are "fairly fast, automatic, irresistible, and highly stimulus-driven." The mapping from pulsation to activity is grounded in the most fundamental biological rhythms: heartbeat, breathing, muscle contraction. This is the encoding **least dependent on cultural convention** and most robust to cross-cultural variation.

**Spatial proximity is well-grounded through Gestalt mechanisms.** The Gestalt principle of proximity (Wertheimer, 1920s) — that nearby items are perceived as related — is one of the most robust perceptual organizing principles identified, and "can overpower competing visual cues such as similarity of color or shape." While most research uses Western samples, the basic perceptual grouping mechanism appears innate. Interpretation of proximity can be culturally inflected, but the fundamental perception of "close items go together" has strong cross-cultural and cross-species support.

**Luminance is moderately grounded in near-universal metaphor.** Meier et al. (2004) demonstrated automatic brightness-positive valence associations. Sherman and Clore (2009) documented a "moral Stroop effect" where color naming was faster for moral words in white and immoral words in black. Chiou and Cheng (2013) replicated brightness-morality effects in Taiwanese contexts, suggesting cross-cultural validity. The GOOD IS LIGHT metaphor (Lakoff & Johnson) appears across Western and Eastern cultures. However, Yang et al. (2017) found some replication failures in Chinese contexts, and the specific mapping from luminance to *system health* (versus goodness generally) adds an additional conventional layer. **Moderate confidence** in cross-cultural grounding, but specific thresholds must be learned.

**Color semantics are definitively not grounded universally.** While Adams and Osgood's 23-culture study found broad trends (white = "good," black = "bad"), specific color-meaning mappings show high cultural variation. Red signifies danger in Western traffic codes but luck and prosperity in Chinese culture. White represents purity in Western contexts but death and mourning in parts of East Asia. A 2023 PMC study on implicit color-valence associations found the red-negative/green-positive association was significantly stronger in Western than Chinese groups. **Color-based encoding must never be the sole carrier of critical meaning** — redundant coding across multiple channels is essential.

**AI grounding remains fundamentally limited.** Harnad's symbol grounding problem (1990) demands that symbols be grounded in non-symbolic sensory representations to avoid infinite regress — "analogous to trying to learn Chinese from a Chinese/Chinese dictionary alone." Current LLMs achieve what might be called **distributional grounding** — word meaning derived from co-occurrence patterns — which Arseniev-Koehler (2024) has shown mirrors Saussurean differential value (meaning from relations rather than reference). But Mollo and Millière (2023) formulate the "Vector Grounding Problem": how can vector embeddings acquire meaning rather than merely relating to other meaningless vectors? AI agents can achieve **functional competence** — correctly mapping luminance to health values and acting appropriately — through operational definitions. This is sufficient for most practical purposes. But genuine understanding in Harnad's sense requires sensorimotor grounding that current architectures lack.

## 9. Computational semiotics supports functional but not genuine AI semiosis

The question of whether AI agents can participate in multi-layered semiotic systems receives a nuanced answer from current research: functional participation is achievable; genuine semiotic understanding is not.

**LLM metaphor processing shows partial capability.** GPT-4 outperforms previous models on metaphor benchmarks, and human judges rated its interpretations of Serbian poetry metaphors as superior to college students' attempts (2024, *Metaphor and Symbol*). However, a comprehensive 2025 evaluation found that "LLMs' performance is more influenced by features like lexical overlap and sentence length than by metaphorical content," suggesting surface-level pattern matching rather than deep understanding. LLMs are prone to confusing source and target domains of metaphors, a fundamental error that genuine metaphor comprehension would not produce.

**Word embeddings implement something remarkably like Saussurean differential value.** Arseniev-Koehler (2024, *Sociological Methods & Research*) demonstrates that word embeddings operationalize three core premises of structural linguistics: meaning is coherent, relational, and analyzable as a static system. This mirrors Saussure's claim that "the content of a sign is ultimately determined not by its internal content, but by what surrounds it." The distributional hypothesis ("you shall know a word by the company it keeps" — Firth) has roots in structuralist thinking. However, embeddings model paradigmatic relations better than syntagmatic ones, and capture public culture rather than individual cognition.

**Emergent communication research shows agents can develop conventions but not full semiotic systems.** Lazaridou et al. (2017) demonstrated that neural agents in referential games develop communication protocols reflecting semantic properties of their perceptual world. Subsequent work has shown compositionality can emerge, larger groups create more systematic languages, and agents can develop discrete symbolic representations autonomously (VQEL, 2024). But emerged languages remain "far from being 'natural'-like" — they lack open vocabulary, complex syntax, and many properties of human language. **True semiotic closure** — agents autonomously generating, interpreting, and evolving novel signs — remains beyond current architectures.

Leone (2023) characterizes AI meaning-production as fundamentally "behavioristic" — functional simulation rather than genuine understanding. For Codex Signum's practical purposes, this may suffice: AI agents can be operationally grounded through explicit algorithmic mappings, processing visual encodings correctly without requiring the embodied, culturally situated understanding that human interpreters bring.

## 10. Cross-cultural design demands redundant coding and empirical testing

The system's visual encodings require a rigorous international testing protocol and cannot rely on assumed universality.

**Vertical spatial metaphors are the safest cross-cultural bet.** The GOOD IS UP metaphor appears nearly universal and does not vary with handedness or writing direction (Casasanto et al. 2022). It has clear embodied basis in upright posture correlating with positive mood. By contrast, horizontal metaphors (GOOD IS RIGHT) vary with both handedness and reading direction — left-handers and RTL readers show reversed patterns. **Design recommendation: favor vertical over horizontal spatial encoding for cross-cultural validity.**

**Reading direction affects all spatial encoding, not just text.** RTL readers perceive temporal graphs differently, showing elevated confusion when temporal flow runs left-to-right. Kress and van Leeuwen's Given/New (left = familiar, right = new) reverses for Arabic and Hebrew visual cultures. This directly undermines any fixed horizontal information value in Codex Signum's graph layouts. Hundreds of millions of people use RTL scripts — this is not a marginal concern.

A testing protocol modeled on ISO 9186-1 methodology should include:

- **Comprehension testing** across minimum 5 culturally distinct populations (Western European, East Asian, Middle Eastern/RTL, Sub-Saharan African, South Asian)
- **Both implicit and explicit measures**, since these can yield contradictory results (the Chinese red-valence finding reversed between explicit and implicit measurement)
- **Reading direction groups** specifically testing LTR, RTL, and traditional TTB readers
- **Literacy level stratification** to validate multi-layered interpretability
- **≥67% comprehension threshold** (ISO 7001 standard) for each encoding type across each cultural group

## Conclusion: theoretically sophisticated, practically vulnerable

Codex Signum demonstrates genuine theoretical sophistication in its semiotic foundations. Its Peircean sign classification, Eco-derived dual-mode encoding, and Barthesian layered signification are well-aligned with established scholarship. The system's most important theoretical contribution — that the same structural encoding can support both overcoded operational precision and undercoded expressive flexibility — is not merely defensible but represents a central insight from Eco's own work.

Three vulnerabilities require immediate attention. First, the **6-morpheme primitive set** is a structural risk. Every historical precedent — from aUI's catastrophic failure with 42 primitives to Blissymbolics' struggles with 100+ — suggests this is insufficient for the claimed expressive range. The system should either expand its primitive inventory to 12-20 elements, reframe its morphemes as parallel encoding dimensions rather than compositional units, or explicitly constrain its semantic domain. Second, **cultural universality claims must be empirically validated**, not assumed. The GOOD IS LIGHT metaphor, while near-universal, has documented failures. Color semantics vary dramatically. Reading direction affects all spatial encoding. The system needs a rigorous cross-cultural testing protocol before claiming global applicability. Third, the **Peircean framework should be explicitly adopted as primary** over the Saussurean one. Saussure's insistence on discreteness and arbitrariness creates persistent friction with continuous encoding and motivated visual signs. Peirce's triadic model, with its accommodation of mixed sign types, continuous properties, and non-linguistic signs, provides a more natural theoretical home for everything Codex Signum is trying to do.

The overall assessment: **the semiotic foundations are theoretically sound in architecture, partially unsupported in universality claims, and practically vulnerable in primitive inventory**. The framework is not misapplying semiotic concepts so much as selectively applying them — using the right theories but occasionally overgeneralizing their scope. Addressing the three identified vulnerabilities would transform Codex Signum from a theoretically interesting design into a semiotically rigorous one.
