|Project|Stated Reason for Failure|Context and Details|Source(s)|
|**Large Language Models (LLMs) Used as Math Tutors** (e.g., GPT-3.5 Turbo, GPT-4o)|**Model Instability and Inconsistency in Intermediate Steps (Technical Risk).**|An evaluation of LLMs used interactively as math tutors found that while they often achieved high final answer accuracy (around 85.5% on problems), only **56.6% of the entire tutoring dialogues were entirely correct**. The core issue was that the models frequently provided **partially incorrect instruction, miscalculated sub-steps, or erroneously flagged correct answers as incorrect**. This technical unreliability in the pedagogical process means that in almost half of interactive sessions, students would receive errors, risking that they would lose trust in the tutor or **develop misconceptions that hinder future learning**. The risk of causing harm to students due to incorrect guidance led the researchers to conduct an offline evaluation instead of using real students.|,,,,.|

2. Project Failure Due to Strategic/Governance Reasons (Solving the Wrong Problem)

|   |   |   |   |
|---|---|---|---|
|Project|Stated Reason for Failure|Context and Details|Source(s)|
|**Discontinued University Chatbot** (Course Registration)|**Poor Scoping/Missing Value and Lack of Governance/Maintenance (Strategic/Organizational).**|A mid-size university implemented a chatbot to handle routine student inquiries related to course registration, aiming to reduce staff call volume. The project was considered a **"complete failure to deliver value"** and functionally stalled.|.|
|**Details of Failure:**|The project's failure was primarily organizational and strategic: 1. **Poor Design/Scoping:** It was built simply by connecting an off-the-shelf bot builder to a **static FAQ webpage**, lacking sophisticated integration with dynamic university systems. This meant it couldn't solve the real, complex, or slightly varied questions students had. 2. **Lack of Maintenance and Governance:** The chatbot was never updated, and **no process or responsibility was assigned for its ongoing improvement**. The stale knowledge base and the **lack of a clear "escalation path"** to a human resulted in a **frustrating user experience**. The overall result was a **negative Return on Investment (ROI)**, as students reverted to calling the help desk, undermining the original objective. This case exemplifies the finding that the most common reason for AI project failure is starting with a technology rather than a **well-defined institutional problem**.|,.||
