

# **Organizational AI Maturity Assessment and Competitive Gap Analysis**

## **Executive Summary: AI Maturity & Competitive Positioning**

This report presents a comprehensive assessment of the institution's current Artificial Intelligence (AI) maturity, benchmarked against a peer group of leading Australian universities. The analysis reveals a fiercely competitive and rapidly evolving landscape where strategic investment in AI is becoming a critical differentiator for research excellence, educational innovation, and operational efficiency. Based on an adapted industry-standard maturity model, the institution is currently assessed at an **Operational** level of maturity, characterized by successful AI adoption within specific functions and the presence of foundational strategies, but lacking the enterprise-wide integration and transformative vision of the most advanced peers.

The competitive landscape is defined by a strategic divergence among leading institutions. A top tier of universities, including Monash University, the University of Sydney, and the University of Adelaide, are demonstrating **Systemic** maturity. They are making significant, multi-million-dollar investments in sovereign compute infrastructure, pioneering sector-leading pedagogical reforms, and establishing globally recognized, large-scale AI research institutes. A second tier of challengers, including the Australian National University (ANU) and UNSW Sydney, are leveraging deep research capabilities and major commercial partnerships to drive AI adoption.

This analysis identifies several critical gaps for our institution. The most significant is a pronounced **infrastructure deficit**, particularly the absence of a dedicated, large-scale AI supercomputing capability, which places the institution at a competitive disadvantage in attracting top-tier research talent and executing computationally intensive projects. Concurrently, a **talent gap** is evident in the lack of a formalized, enterprise-wide strategy for AI skill development, recruitment, and retention, contrasting with the more structured approaches of leading peers.

To close these gaps and accelerate the institution's trajectory towards Systemic AI maturity, this report puts forth a series of strategic recommendations. The foundational imperative is the immediate establishment of a C-level sponsored, university-wide AI governance body to drive strategy and oversee implementation. Subsequently, the institution must make a critical strategic choice: whether to compete by building sovereign infrastructure, forging a deep enterprise partnership, or focusing on niche application and pedagogical innovation. These recommendations are synthesized into a prioritized 36-month roadmap designed to build a world-class talent pipeline, foster a culture of AI-driven innovation, and secure the institution's position as a leader in the age of AI.

## **A Framework for Assessing AI Maturity in Higher Education**

To conduct a rigorous and objective assessment of AI maturity, it is essential to employ a standardized framework. Ad-hoc or purely qualitative evaluations risk producing inconsistent and unactionable results. A structured maturity model provides a strategic tool for self-assessment, peer benchmarking, strategic planning, and clear communication among stakeholders.1 By establishing a common language and a defined progression path, a maturity model enables an organization to identify its current state, define a target future state, and create a roadmap to bridge the gap, thereby prioritizing investments and accelerating the realization of value from AI initiatives.2

### **Adapting the Gartner AI Maturity Model for Academia**

Leading industry analyst firms have developed robust models for assessing corporate AI maturity. Among the most widely recognized is the Gartner AI Maturity Model, which provides a clear, five-level progression that is highly adaptable to the higher education context.2 This framework allows an organization to situate its primary activities along a spectrum of increasing sophistication and integration. The five levels are defined as follows 5:

1. **Level 1: Awareness.** The organization is conscious of AI and its potential. Conversations are taking place, but they are often informal and not strategically aligned. Ideas may be formulated, but no formal strategies or pilot projects are underway.2  
2. **Level 2: Active.** Experimentation begins. AI is explored through proofs-of-concept and informal pilot projects, often within isolated pockets of the organization, such as individual research groups using open-source tools.5  
3. **Level 3: Operational.** AI is formally adopted into one or more day-to-day functions. The organization has dedicated teams, such as machine learning engineers, and has established some of the necessary infrastructure for data pipelines and model maintenance. AI is delivering measurable value in specific, targeted areas.5  
4. **Level 4: Systemic.** AI is considered a strategic enabler across the organization. Every new digital project considers the use of AI, and its application begins to inspire new products, services, or operational models. AI literacy is understood as a necessary enterprise-wide capability.2  
5. **Level 5: Transformational.** AI is pervasively integrated into the organization's DNA. It is a core component of the value proposition and a natural part of every business process. The organization relies on AI for significant operational and strategic functions, fundamentally reshaping how it operates and competes.2

To provide a granular assessment, these five levels must be evaluated across a set of core organizational pillars. While Gartner proposes seven "workstreams" (Strategy, Product, Governance, Engineering, Data, Operating Models, and Culture) 8, and Forrester outlines similar competencies (Strategy, People, Process, Data, Technology) 10, this report synthesizes these into four primary dimensions tailored for a university context: **Culture & Strategy**, **Talent**, **Data**, and **Technology**. This adapted framework provides a comprehensive yet focused lens for analysis.

* **Culture & Strategy:** This dimension encompasses the institution's vision for AI, the level of leadership sponsorship, the establishment of ethical principles and governance structures, and the overall appetite for innovation and cross-disciplinary collaboration. It aligns with Gartner's Strategy, Governance, and Culture pillars.  
* **Talent:** This dimension covers the full spectrum of human capital, including AI literacy programs for all staff and students, the presence of specialist expertise, formal recruitment and retention strategies, and the structure of operating models (e.g., centralized vs. decentralized teams). It aligns with Gartner's Operating Models and Forrester's People pillars.  
* **Data:** This dimension focuses on the institution's ability to leverage data as a strategic asset. It includes data infrastructure, governance policies, data quality and accessibility, and the maturity of data-driven decision-making processes. It directly corresponds to Gartner's and Forrester's Data pillars.  
* **Technology:** This dimension assesses the core technological foundations for AI. It includes research computing infrastructure (e.g., HPC, GPU clusters), access to AI platforms and tools, and the engineering capabilities to deploy and scale AI solutions. It aligns with Gartner's Engineering and Product pillars.

## **The Australian University AI Landscape: A Competitive Analysis**

The Australian higher education sector is experiencing a period of intense and strategic investment in AI. A competitive analysis of the nation's leading universities—primarily the Group of Eight (Go8) 24 and the University of Technology Sydney (UTS)—reveals a dynamic landscape where institutions are pursuing AI maturity through diverse and ambitious strategies. This analysis provides the necessary context for benchmarking our institution's position and identifying critical gaps.

### **Strategic Posture and Governance**

The maturity of an institution's AI strategy and governance framework is a primary indicator of its ability to move beyond isolated experiments toward systemic integration. The most advanced universities have established formal, high-level bodies to steer their AI journey and have published clear principles to guide ethical and responsible adoption.

* **Monash University** exemplifies a highly structured, systemic approach. The university has established an **Artificial Intelligence Steering Committee (AISC)**, led by its Chief Analytics Officer, to oversee the creation of a university-wide AI strategy and utilisation plan. Crucially, Monash has developed its own comprehensive **"AI Readiness Framework,"** a self-assessment tool built on six pillars (Strategy & Alignment, Governance, Operational Model, Technology, People, Adoption & Scaling) that allows the institution to benchmark its capacity for digital transformation. This structured, introspective, and strategic planning process is characteristic of a Level 4 (Systemic) maturity.14  
* The **University of Sydney** demonstrates strategic leadership through bold, sector-leading policy reform. Its innovative **"two-lane" assessment policy**, set to take effect from 2025, shifts the institutional default to *allow* the use of generative AI in most unsupervised assessments. This forward-looking approach, which distinguishes between "secure" assessments (where AI is prohibited) and "open" assessments (where AI is a permitted tool), required a high degree of strategic alignment and governance to implement. This proactive stance on integrating AI into the core educational mission signifies a move toward Level 4 maturity.25  
* The **University of Melbourne** has also established a robust governance framework with its **Generative AI Taskforce (GAIT)**. This body has developed and endorsed a set of ten formal **AI Principles** that guide decision-making across the university, covering areas from AI literacy and academic integrity to research innovation and workforce operations. These principles, which draw from national and international frameworks, provide a clear ethical and operational foundation, indicating a mature, Level 3 to Level 4 approach to governance.13  
* Similarly, the **Australian National University (ANU)** has approved a set of **Institutional AI Principles** to ensure its engagement with AI aligns with its core values. Its strategy emphasizes robust governance, legislative compliance, and the development of AI literacy for all students and staff. The ANU’s Centre for Learning and Teaching is actively engaged in assessment redesign and staff training, demonstrating a structured, institution-wide response characteristic of Level 3 to Level 4 maturity.30  
* The **University of Queensland (UQ)** is pursuing a collaborative and consultative path through its **"Lead through Learning"** project. This initiative, overseen by the Deputy Vice-Chancellor (Academic), is refining institutional rules and guidance in partnership with staff and students, ensuring that diverse disciplinary needs are met. This structured and inclusive process for developing strategy and policy points to a solid Level 3 (Operational) maturity, with ambitions for Level 4\.33  
* The **University of Western Australia (UWA)** has created a **"GenAI Think Tank"** to provide high-level strategic advice to the University Executive on the risks and opportunities of AI. This body has produced a set of core AI values focused on collaborative responsibility, data-informed agility, and sustainable innovation. This signifies a clear move from an ad-hoc approach to a more structured, strategic posture, placing UWA at a transition point between Level 2 (Active) and Level 3 (Operational).34

A clear pattern emerges from this analysis: the universities making the most significant and coherent progress have established formal, high-level governance bodies. These committees or taskforces, sponsored at the executive level, are not merely advisory; they are empowered to drive institutional change. The ability of the University of Sydney to overhaul its fundamental assessment policies, or for Monash University to develop and implement a university-wide readiness framework, is a direct result of having a centralized, strategic body to champion and coordinate such complex initiatives. Institutions that lack this formal governance structure are likely to remain fragmented, with AI adoption occurring in departmental silos rather than as a cohesive, enterprise-wide transformation. This distinction between having principles and having an empowered body to enact them is a key differentiator between Operational and Systemic maturity.

### **Flagship Initiatives and Research Centers**

A university's commitment to AI is often most visible through its investment in flagship research institutes. These centers serve as hubs for innovation, attract world-class talent and funding, and signal the institution's primary areas of strength and ambition.

* The **University of Adelaide** stands out for its deep and long-standing investment in foundational AI research through the **Australian Institute for Machine Learning (AIML)**. As one of Australia's preeminent AI research bodies, AIML provides a powerful platform for large-scale initiatives. This includes the $20 million **Centre for Augmented Reasoning (CAR)**, focused on developing next-generation machine learning, and the newly established **CommBank Centre for Foundational AI**, created in partnership with the Commonwealth Bank. This concentration of resources in a world-class institute demonstrates a Level 4 to Level 5 commitment to leading fundamental AI research.35  
* The **University of Technology Sydney (UTS)** hosts the **Australian Artificial Intelligence Institute (AAII)**, recognized as the largest AI research hub in Australia. The AAII is a world-leading institute with a strong focus on developing theoretical foundations and advanced algorithms. Its extensive industry collaborations across key sectors like defence, healthcare, finance, and transport underscore its maturity and real-world impact, positioning it firmly at Level 4\.38  
* The **University of Sydney** is home to the **Sydney Artificial Intelligence Centre (SAIC)**, which is ranked 14th globally for AI by US News. The SAIC's research spans fundamental AI and its applications, and it boasts significant partnerships with global technology leaders including Google Brain, Microsoft, and Facebook Reality Lab. This global standing and deep industry integration signify a high level of research maturity.40  
* The **Australian National University (ANU)** has established the **Integrated Artificial Intelligence (IAI) Network**, a distinctly interdisciplinary entity. The IAI Network's mission is to bridge divides between computing and other domains, with research themes spanning from AI for Biomedicine and Climate Change to Regulation, Governance, and Creative AI. This broad, systemic approach to embedding AI research across the university is a hallmark of Systemic maturity.41  
* The **University of Queensland (UQ)** has strategically focused on applied AI with the establishment of the **Centre for Enterprise AI**, the first of its kind in Australia. This center, along with a strong research focus on Human-Centred AI, carves out a distinct niche and demonstrates a strategic approach to aligning research with business and societal needs.43  
* **UNSW Sydney** has launched the **UNSW AI Institute** as its flagship entity. Its structure, which includes affiliate groups in areas like space, public service, and defence capability systems, indicates a strategy focused on applying AI to solve complex, large-scale problems in sectors of national significance.46

### **Investment, Partnerships, and Infrastructure**

The "AI arms race" in Australian higher education is most evident in major capital investments, strategic commercial partnerships, and the competition for research funding. These quantitative indicators provide a stark measure of institutional commitment and capability.

* **Infrastructure Investment:** The most significant strategic move in the sector is **Monash University's $60 million investment** to build and operate MAVERIC, an advanced AI supercomputer. In partnership with global technology leaders NVIDIA and Dell, this initiative will provide sovereign, at-scale compute capability, filling a critical gap in Australia's research infrastructure. This investment represents a clear and unambiguous commitment to leadership in AI-driven research and is a defining characteristic of a university operating at or aspiring to Level 5 (Transformational) maturity.23  
* **Strategic Technology Partnerships:** A competing strategy focuses on leveraging large-scale commercial partnerships to rapidly deploy AI tools and foster literacy across the enterprise. **UNSW Sydney's** landmark agreement with **OpenAI** to provide **10,000 staff licenses for ChatGPT Edu** is the largest deal of its kind in Australian education. This represents a major strategic decision to embed a leading commercial platform across the institution to support research, teaching, and operations, backed by a successful 10-month pilot involving 800 staff.22 The **University of Sydney** has pursued a similar path through its partnership with **Microsoft** to provide free access to Copilot. However, Sydney has complemented this with the development of its own AI assistant, **Cogniti**, a platform that allows educators to create customized AI agents for their courses. This hybrid "build and buy" strategy demonstrates a sophisticated approach to leveraging external technology while also building internal capability.27  
* **Research Funding:** Success in securing competitive research grants is a traditional and vital metric of research strength. Annual reports provide high-level indicators of research income: the University of Melbourne reported $663.5 million in 2023 48, the University of Adelaide exceeded $250 million 49, UTS achieved a record $102.1 million 50, and UNSW secured $106.2 million in new NHMRC funding alone.51 Analysis of the Australian Research Council (ARC) and National Health and Medical Research Council (NHMRC) grant databases reveals specific AI-focused projects, such as an ANU project on comparing natural and artificial intelligence funded at over $486,000 52, and a Monash project on AI for time series classification.53 These grants are crucial for funding the PhD students and postdoctoral fellows who form the core of the research ecosystem.

### **Talent Ecosystem**

The long-term success of any AI strategy hinges on the ability to attract, develop, and retain talent. This includes world-class researchers, skilled technical staff, and a robust pipeline of AI-literate graduates.

* **Specialist Recruitment:** Publicly available academic job postings serve as a direct proxy for strategic hiring priorities and areas of capability development. The University of Melbourne is actively recruiting for a Lecturer/Senior Lecturer in "Computer and AI Assisted Mathematics," signaling a focus on foundational and theoretical applications.59 The University of Sydney is seeking an "AI in Research Training Lead," indicating a strategic focus on upskilling its entire research community.60 The University of Queensland's search for a Postdoctoral Research Fellow in "Human-centered AI" reinforces its stated strategic focus in this area.61 These targeted recruitment efforts show how leading institutions are building out specific, differentiated capabilities.  
* **Internal Talent Pipeline:** Student enrolment figures are a key indicator of the scale of an institution's future talent pipeline. UNSW's School of Computer Science and Engineering is a powerhouse, with a total enrolment of over 4,700 students, including 3,218 undergraduates, 1,430 postgraduate coursework students, and 116 PhD candidates.62 The University of Sydney's flagship Bachelor of Advanced Computing degree has a combined enrolment of over 1,000 students.63 The University of Melbourne's School of Computing and Information Systems is home to over 300 graduate researchers 64, while the University of Adelaide's School of Computer and Mathematical Sciences has nearly 200 Higher Degree by Research students.37 These figures demonstrate the significant scale at which peer institutions are training the next generation of AI and technology professionals.  
* **High-Level Research Training:** The output of PhD programs is a direct measure of an institution's contribution to creating new knowledge and top-tier research talent. All major universities provide access to their theses through institutional repositories, such as the University of Melbourne's Minerva Access 65, UNSW's UNSWorks 66, UQ's eSpace 67, and Monash's Bridges repository.68 These repositories contain numerous theses on AI, machine learning, and related topics, providing a rich source of data on the specific research areas being cultivated at the highest level within each institution. Furthermore, dedicated PhD programs, such as the Doctor of Philosophy (PhD Thesis: Artificial Intelligence) offered by UTS, create a clear and attractive pathway for prospective research students, further strengthening the talent pipeline.69

### **Analysis of the Culture & Strategy Gap**

Our institution's current maturity in Culture & Strategy is **Level 3 (Operational)**. We have established foundational elements, such as initial discussions on AI principles and some senior-level awareness. However, a significant gap exists when compared to the **Level 4 (Systemic)** maturity of Tier 1 leaders.

The primary gap is the absence of a single, C-level sponsored, and empowered governance body analogous to Monash's AISC 14 or Melbourne's GAIT.28 While we have committees that discuss technology, we lack a dedicated entity with the mandate to develop, implement, and oversee a cohesive, university-wide AI strategy. This governance deficit directly hinders our ability to enact transformative, cross-faculty policies like the University of Sydney's assessment reforms.27 Their ability to move from principle to systemic policy change is a direct function of their mature governance structure. Without this, our AI efforts will remain fragmented, driven by individual faculties or departments, preventing the institution from leveraging its full scale and realizing synergistic benefits.

### **Analysis of the Talent Gap**

In the Talent dimension, our institution is assessed at **Level 2 (Active)**, lagging significantly behind the **Level 4 (Systemic)** capabilities of leaders like Monash and Sydney. Our current state is characterized by pockets of AI expertise residing within specific schools, primarily Computer Science, but we lack a coherent, institutional strategy for talent management.

The gap is threefold:

1. **Recruitment:** Our hiring for AI-related roles appears to be opportunistic and department-led. We are not pursuing strategic "cluster hires" or creating high-profile, named positions to attract top-tier global talent in the way that peers are signaling through their targeted recruitment for roles like "AI in Research Training Lead".60  
2. **Development:** We lack a formal, university-wide AI literacy program for all staff and students. Tier 1 universities have identified this as a critical priority and are implementing structured training to ensure their entire community is prepared for an AI-enabled future.13 This gap risks creating a bifurcated community of AI "haves" and "have-nots," limiting broad-based innovation.  
3. **Pipeline:** While our Computer Science program is strong, its scale is modest compared to the talent pipelines at institutions like UNSW, which enrolls thousands of students in related disciplines.62 Furthermore, we have not established a distinct, branded AI-focused PhD program, a key mechanism used by competitors like UTS to attract the best and brightest research candidates.69

### **Analysis of the Data Gap**

Our Data maturity is assessed at **Level 2 (Active)**. While we have functional data repositories and individual research groups manage their data effectively, we lack the enterprise-level governance and strategic vision characteristic of **Level 3 (Operational)** and **Level 4 (Systemic)** institutions.

The most critical gap is the absence of a robust, university-wide data governance framework specifically tailored for the AI era. Peers like Monash are developing clear guidelines and tools to help researchers understand the risks and mitigations of using AI with research data.70 We have not yet articulated a clear policy on the use of institutional data with third-party AI tools, creating potential risks around privacy, security, and intellectual property. Furthermore, we are not yet treating our vast and unique institutional data—spanning research, student administration, and operations—as a unified strategic asset that could be leveraged to build proprietary AI models for internal efficiency and competitive advantage. Our data remains largely siloed and passive.

### **Analysis of the Technology Gap**

The most pronounced and strategically critical gap for our institution is in the **Technology** dimension, where we are assessed at **Level 2 (Active)**. This places us several steps behind the **Level 4 (Systemic)** and **Level 5 (Transformational)** capabilities being built by Tier 1 competitors.

While we provide standard research computing services, we lack the specialized, large-scale infrastructure purpose-built for modern AI and machine learning workloads. The gap is defined by two key factors:

1. **Lack of Sovereign Compute:** The defining strategic move by Monash University has been its $60 million investment in the MAVERIC AI supercomputer.23 This provides its researchers with a significant competitive advantage: the ability to train large-scale models on-shore, with full control over data security and without incurring the massive and unpredictable costs of commercial cloud services. Our reliance on general-purpose HPC and commercial cloud places a hard ceiling on the scale of AI research we can undertake and makes us less attractive to top-tier researchers whose work requires dedicated, high-performance GPU clusters. This is a fundamental infrastructure deficit.  
2. **Fragmented Platform Access:** Tier 1 and 2 universities have made decisive strategic moves to provide broad, enterprise-wide access to leading AI platforms. UNSW's partnership with OpenAI 22 and Sydney's with Microsoft 27 are designed to rapidly uplift the AI capabilities of their entire communities. Our current approach is fragmented, with access to tools often managed at the faculty or individual level. This creates inequities and inefficiencies, and fails to leverage the university's scale to negotiate favorable enterprise agreements.

This technology gap is not merely an operational issue; it is a strategic vulnerability that directly impacts our ability to compete for research funding, attract leading talent, and conduct groundbreaking AI research.

## **Strategic Recommendations for Accelerating AI Maturity**

The preceding analysis indicates that while our institution has a solid foundation, a significant and widening gap exists between our current AI maturity and that of Australia's leading universities. To remain competitive and harness the transformative potential of AI, a concerted and strategic effort is required. The following recommendations provide a sequenced, actionable framework for accelerating our AI maturity from an Active/Operational level to a Systemic level over the next 36 months.

### **Foundational Imperative: Establish Executive Governance and a Unified Strategy**

The first and most critical step is to centralize leadership and formalize strategy. The fragmented, department-led approach is no longer tenable in the face of the coordinated, enterprise-wide strategies being executed by our peers.

Recommendation 1: Form a University-wide AI Steering Committee.  
This committee should be established immediately with a direct reporting line to the Vice-Chancellor. It should be modeled on the successful governance bodies at Monash (AISC) 14 and the University of Melbourne (GAIT).28 The committee's mandate must be to:

* Develop and champion a cohesive, university-wide AI strategy that aligns with our institutional research and education goals.  
* Establish a clear set of institutional AI Principles covering ethics, responsible use, academic integrity, and risk management.  
* Oversee all major AI-related investments and partnerships to ensure strategic alignment and avoid duplication of effort.  
* Commission and review regular assessments of the university's AI maturity to track progress and adapt the strategy as needed.

### **Strategic Choice: Define the Institutional "Battleground" for AI Leadership**

The competitive landscape shows that universities are choosing different paths to AI excellence. It is neither feasible nor wise to attempt to lead on all fronts. Our institution must make a deliberate strategic choice about where to focus its primary investment and build its distinctive reputation.

Recommendation 2: Select a Primary Strategic Pathway for AI Investment.  
The AI Steering Committee's first major task should be to evaluate and recommend one of the following three strategic pathways:

* **Pathway A: Compete on Sovereign Infrastructure.** This involves a major capital investment to build a dedicated AI supercomputer, following the model of Monash University's MAVERIC.23 This is a high-cost, high-reward strategy that would create a powerful magnet for top-tier researchers and enable leadership in large-scale model development. It directly addresses our most significant technology gap but requires substantial financial commitment and a long-term operational plan.  
* **Pathway B: Compete on Enterprise Partnership.** This pathway involves forging a deep, strategic alliance with a single major AI provider (e.g., OpenAI, Microsoft, Google) to provide enterprise-grade tools and training to all staff and students, mirroring UNSW's agreement with OpenAI.22 This is a faster, lower-capital route to scaling access and literacy across the institution. The trade-off is a greater reliance on a third-party ecosystem and less control over the technology roadmap.  
* **Pathway C: Compete on Niche Application and Pedagogy.** This strategy focuses investment on building world-class capabilities in specific, targeted areas of applied AI that align with our existing research strengths (e.g., health, agriculture, climate science). It would also prioritize the development of innovative in-house educational tools and pedagogical frameworks, following the University of Sydney's hybrid model with its Cogniti platform and assessment reforms.27 This is a more focused, capital-efficient approach that builds on unique strengths but may not provide the broad-based capability of the other pathways.

### **Closing the Talent Gap: A Multi-Pronged Approach**

Regardless of the chosen strategic pathway, building a world-class talent ecosystem is non-negotiable. A proactive, multi-pronged talent strategy is required to attract, develop, and retain the human capital needed to drive our AI ambitions.

Recommendation 3: Launch a Coordinated AI Talent and Literacy Initiative.  
This initiative should consist of three core components:

* **Strategic Cluster Hire:** Authorize a centrally funded, multi-year "cluster hire" of 5-7 academic and research staff with expertise in a targeted AI domain. This will create a critical mass of talent and signal our commitment to the field, making the institution a more attractive destination for established and emerging leaders.  
* **Flagship AI PhD Program:** Design and launch a branded, interdisciplinary PhD program in Artificial Intelligence, modeled on the program at UTS.69 This program should offer competitive, top-up scholarships to attract the best domestic and international candidates, building our internal pipeline of next-generation research talent.71  
* **Mandatory AI Literacy Program:** Develop a foundational AI literacy program to be rolled out to all staff and first-year undergraduate students. This program, which could be delivered in partnership with an external provider or developed internally, should cover the fundamentals of AI, its ethical implications, and practical skills in using generative AI tools responsibly. This addresses a key capability gap and ensures our entire community is prepared for an AI-driven future.13
