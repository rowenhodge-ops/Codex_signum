**(Context: Differentiating Project ROI vs. Competitive Strategy. The 'Soft ROI' insight.)**

The sources define **Soft ROI (Return on Investment)** as the primary measure of value for AI projects, prioritizing **mission-centric outcomes** over simple cost-cutting, as stipulated in the required **Hybrid ROI Model**.

Here are five key metrics used to measure the 'Soft ROI' or 'Mission-Critical Value' of an AI project in a university context:

1. Student Retention and Persistence Rates

This metric tracks the AI project's direct contribution to maintaining and improving the student body. AI is deployed in this area primarily through **predictive analytics** and **early intervention systems** to identify students at risk of falling behind or dropping out.

• **Metric Focus:** The percentage of students who persist, complete a course, or re-enroll.

• **Target Example:** Reducing the percentage of students who drop off at a specified milestone (e.g., census date).

• **Rationale:** Proactive student support, delivered through tools like chatbots and predictive analytics, is proven to enhance student success and graduation outcomes. The Early Intervention and Support Initiative (EISI) is a clear example of proactively supporting students identified as at risk of disengagement.

2. Faculty/Staff Time Reclaimed (Operational Efficiency)

This metric quantifies the human capital freed up when AI automates routine or repetitive administrative tasks, allowing skilled personnel to focus on higher-value activities like research, teaching, mentoring, and strategic decision-making.

• **Metric Focus:** The reduction in full-time equivalent (FTE) hours or total hours per week spent on administrative tasks.

• **Target Example:** Moving from "X hours/week on admin" (Baseline) to freeing "Y hours for research/teaching" (Target). Also measured by the reduction in low-level Tier-1 inquiry emails or calls to service desks.

• **Rationale:** Projects that reduce the **administrative burden on faculty and staff** are identified as strategic initial "Quick Wins" because they deliver immediate goodwill and build the human capacity needed for later, more complex pedagogical innovations.

3. Student Satisfaction and Engagement Scores

This measure evaluates the quality of the student experience and the successful adoption of AI tools, which is crucial for overall institutional quality performance.

• **Metric Focus:** Stakeholder satisfaction metrics, often captured through user surveys, and specific measures of course engagement.

• **Target Example:** Increasing the average score on the Student Evaluation of Course and Teacher (SECaT) platform or similar feedback mechanism by a defined percentage.

• **Rationale:** AI applications enhance the overall student experience by providing **24/7 accessibility to support services** and personalized content. AI-driven assessments can also be used to amplify the student voice.

4. Research Productivity and Acceleration (Time-to-Discovery)

This metric assesses the impact of AI on the university’s mission to generate and disseminate knowledge by leveraging AI tools to accelerate research activities.

• **Metric Focus:** Reduction in the time required to complete research steps, such as literature reviews, data analysis, or proposal preparation.

• **Target Example:** Average time required to reach a key discovery reduced (Baseline vs. Post-Implementation).

• **Rationale:** AI assists in analyzing vast academic and scientific datasets to identify trends and patterns and can be used to generate first drafts of grant proposals or summarize complex literature. Investments in AI are predicted to return **increased research productivity** and grant competitiveness.

5. Ethical Compliance Rate and Risk Avoidance

This metric measures the effectiveness of the AI governance framework, which is a mission-critical function tied to accountability and institutional reputation.

• **Metric Focus:** Ensuring all new AI initiatives undergo mandatory ethical screening and risk assessment before deployment.

• **Target Example:** **100% of new projects assessed** against the institutional ethical principles. Alternatively, minimizing operational risk, such as tracking the number of governance-related errors or high-severity internal audit findings.

• **Rationale:** Mitigating ethical failures, such as algorithmic bias and data breaches, is crucial for maintaining public trust and avoiding reputational damage. Ethical principles, clear accountabilities, and transparency are necessary components of responsible AI use.
