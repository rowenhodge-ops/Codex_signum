### **Node: `path-A2-level-3`**

**(Context: Project scoping. Vetting a specific idea.)**

The sources detail several complementary frameworks for scoping, vetting, and prioritizing new AI projects, recognizing that robust governance and strategy must precede technology deployment.

The most actionable framework presented for planning and vetting a new project is the **Dual-Framework Methodology**, which uses a **Value vs. Complexity Prioritisation Matrix** followed by a deeper **AI-3P Readiness Assessment**. These frameworks move projects from a long list of possibilities to a defensible shortlist of strategic priorities.

Framework for Scoping and Vetting New AI Projects

1. Framework 1: The Value vs. Complexity Prioritization Matrix

This initial stage uses a high-level filtering process to categorize potential use cases based on their potential benefit versus the effort and risk required for implementation. It sorts initiatives into four distinct, actionable quadrants:

• **Quick Wins (High Value, Low Complexity):** Top priorities for immediate action, ideal for building early momentum and demonstrating value.

• **Strategic Initiatives (High Value, High Complexity):** Transformational projects requiring substantial planning and long-term commitment (candidates for later phases).

• **Fill-Ins (Low Value, Low Complexity):** Opportunistic projects that should not distract from higher priorities.

• **Reconsider (Low Value, High Complexity):** Initiatives that should be actively avoided due to high resource consumption and low strategic gain.

2. Framework 2: The AI-3P Readiness Assessment

For initiatives categorized as high-value ("Quick Wins" and "Strategic Initiatives"), this framework provides a second, deeper level of analysis to evaluate the institution's concrete, holistic readiness to execute successfully. This assessment scores the project against three capacity pillars:

• **Pillar 1: People:** Assesses the human element, including clear executive sponsorship, necessary skills (data science, ML operations), and a plan for end-user adoption and change management.

• **Pillar 2: Process:** Evaluates the operational fit, ensuring the solution integrates with existing workflows, complies with regulations, and has clear governance and ongoing support processes.

• **Pillar 3: Product:** Challenges technical assumptions regarding the maturity and scalability of the underlying technology, quality of the end-user experience, and financial viability (Total Cost of Ownership).

The outcome is a quantitative readiness score, resulting in a decision to either **Build Now**, proceed to **Pilot with Guardrails**, or **De-risk First**.

Key Criteria for a 'Good' First AI Project (Quick Wins)

A "good" first project is strategically defined as a **Minimum Viable AI Product (MVAIP)**, which should fall into the **Quick Wins (High Value, Low Complexity)** quadrant of the matrix.

The key criteria for scoping a successful first project are focused on achieving high impact while mitigating the risk factors that commonly cause the **80% failure rate**:

|---|---|---|
|Criteria Dimension|Key Criteria for a Good First Project (MVAIP)|Source(s)|
|**Value (High ROI)**|**Clear Strategic Value:** Must solve a **specific institutional problem**, not just use a cool technology. Projects should target solving immediate, **deeply felt pain points** for faculty or staff, which generates immediate goodwill and frees human capital.||
|**Complexity (Low Investment/Effort)**|**Minimal Investment/Resource Constraints:** The project should require lower estimated financial costs and development hours.||
|**Measurable Impact (Fast Feedback)**|**Quick to Show Value:** The initiative should allow results and impact to be measured quickly. This facilitates **rapid learning** and allows the project to serve as a positive case study for the entire organization.||
|**Data Readiness (Low Risk)**|**Low Data Sensitivity/High Availability:** Requires minimal institutional data or uses non-sensitive data. A good first project should **not** be crippled by data quality issues.||
|**Risk/Governance**|**Low Inherent Risk:** Should be a **Low-Risk** project (e.g., internal process automation, non-PII data) that can be reviewed by a Faculty/Unit AI Review Board, rather than escalating to the central AI Steering Committee. A critical risk is public failure, so using internal-facing tools is preferred.||
|**Scalability**|**Scalability Potential:** While starting small, the project must be designed with the **bigger picture in mind**, ensuring it can scale or integrate into broader systems later, rather than creating a proof-of-concept that cannot be extended.||

The most common examples of MVAIPs recommended for Year 1 initiation include AI-assisted tools for faculty (e.g., course material generators) and administrative chatbots for routine inquiries (e.g., HR or Financial Aid).
