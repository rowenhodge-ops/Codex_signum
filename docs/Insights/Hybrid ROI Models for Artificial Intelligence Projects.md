**(Context: Differentiating Project ROI vs. Competitive Strategy. The 'Soft ROI' insight.)**


The traditional Return on Investment (ROI) models often fail for Artificial Intelligence (AI) projects because they **overlook the mission-critical, non-financial benefits** that AI primarily delivers, and they struggle to account for the unique characteristics and risks of AI implementation.

The conventional approach, focused narrowly on quantifiable financial metrics (Hard ROI), is deemed **insufficient for AI**.

Quote or Summary Explaining the Failure of Traditional ROI Models

The core issue is summarized by the required adoption of a **Hybrid ROI Model** for project evaluation:

**“A simple cost-benefit analysis is insufficient for AI, as it often overlooks critical factors like organizational readiness and ethical risk. Therefore, a more nuanced, two-stage methodology is required to move from the long list of possibilities to a defensible shortlist of strategic priorities.”**

Detailed Reasons for the Insufficiency of Traditional ROI

The sources advocate for a **Hybrid ROI Model** because traditional models fail to capture the following crucial aspects of AI value creation:

1. **Prioritization of Non-Financial, Mission-Centric Value (Soft ROI):** AI projects, particularly in higher education, often generate their greatest value not in immediate cost savings but in **mission-centric outcomes**. These include **improving student retention**, **enhancing the student or faculty experience**, and **reclaiming faculty/staff time** for higher-value work. The success of a project is primarily measured by these mission-critical metrics. For instance, a "Soft ROI" project, like the Georgia State University chatbot, proved that enhanced student support could deliver the **strongest "Hard ROI" outcome** (a 3.3% enrollment increase), demonstrating that mission value drives financial uplift.

2. **Inclusion of Risk and Complexity:** Traditional ROI models treat costs and benefits linearly. AI projects, however, must factor in the **Complexity Axis**, which accounts for inherent ethical or privacy risks, technical integration difficulty, and organizational disruption. Ignoring these factors—which are central to AI's **80% failure rate**—renders the financial assessment meaningless.

3. **Accounting for Non-Linear Value and Uncertainty:** The return on investment from AI is often **non-linear and accelerates over time**. Machine Learning models inherently improve as they process more data, leading to **exponentially compounding value** that is not captured by static, traditional calculations. Furthermore, the complexity and uncertainty of AI projects mean that cost estimates are often very tricky to determine upfront, forcing the use of a "faith-based business case" or a **qualitative** rather than a purely **quantitative** justification.

4. **Strategic Alignment:** The effectiveness of AI integration relies on alignment with **strategic business goals** and **leadership involvement**, rather than simply individual user perception of ease of use or usefulness. A financial-only model cannot measure this necessary strategic alignment.

Therefore, organizations are mandated to use a **Value Realization Plan** that defines both Hard ROI (financial metrics like cost avoidance and enrollment) and Soft ROI (mission-critical metrics) before a project is approved, ensuring value is tracked holistically.
