Checklist and Principles for Starting a New AI Project

The frameworks provided in the sources—including the AI Governance Checklist, the AI Project Charter (MVAIP), and the foundational AI Principles—offer a comprehensive guide for starting an AI project.

A. Core Principles for Project Design (The Ethical and Operational Guardrails)

Before technical work begins, every AI project must align with foundational principles to ensure responsible and trustworthy use.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Principle|Commitment / Requirement|Source(s)|
|**Beneficial / Human-Centric**|Ensure the AI serves the **best interests of people** (students, staff, society) and that the likely benefits substantially outweigh foreseeable risks. Prioritize human well-being and fundamental human rights.||
|**Fair & Inclusive**|Proactively **identify, measure, and mitigate unfair bias** in data and models to avoid unjust impacts on individuals or groups. Ensure equitable access to AI tools.||
|**Transparent & Explainable**|Ensure users **know when they are interacting with an AI** and that the reasoning behind AI-driven decisions is understandable and accessible to those affected.||
|**Accountable & Contestable**|Establish **clear lines of human responsibility** and accountability for AI outputs. Provide clear, accessible avenues for individuals to **question, challenge, and seek review** of AI-driven outcomes (Contestability).||
|**Privacy-Preserving & Secure**|**Safeguard sensitive data** in strict compliance with relevant laws and implement privacy-by-design principles. Use data minimization principles.||
|**Reliable & Safe**|Rigorously **test systems for security, accuracy, and performance** to prevent unintended or harmful outcomes. Ensure the system is **robust** against various failures and attacks.||

B. Project Initiation Checklist (MVAIP Charter Requirements)

For an individual project (such as a Minimum Viable AI Product, or MVAIP), the project lead must secure approval by completing mandatory documentation that answers key questions about scope, value, and risk.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Checklist Component|Key Requirements and Questions to be Answered|Source(s)|
|**Strategic Alignment & Problem Definition**|**What specific institutional problem does this project solve?** Why is AI well-suited for this problem over traditional software? Define measurable success metrics (**Hard ROI and Soft ROI**) in a Value Realization Plan.||
|**Governance & Risk Assessment**|Complete the **AI Project Risk Assessment Form**. Define the **Data Classification** (Tier 1-4). Determine if the project is low, medium, or high risk, and thus which **AI Review Board** must approve it. Document adherence to all core ethical principles.||
|**Scope & Resources**|Clearly define what is **in scope** and **explicitly excluded** (scope creep mitigation). Outline the major data sources that will be needed, noting potential challenges with availability or quality. Define roles and responsibilities, identifying the designated **Project Lead** and **Project Sponsor**.||
|**Human Oversight & Review**|Describe the **human oversight and intervention mechanism** (e.g., human-in-the-loop). Specify the **process for users to appeal or challenge** the AI’s output (Contestability).||

The Most Critical First Step

The sources overwhelmingly indicate that the most critical first step for initiating an AI strategy or project is establishing the **strategic foundation and governance structure**, which begins with **defining the mission-aligned vision and ethical principles**.

The sources provide strong arguments against starting with technology or data validation alone:

1. **Strategic Vision and Alignment:** The single most critical step for the overall strategic roadmap is to **articulate a clear, compelling, and mission-aligned vision for AI**. This vision serves as the "North Star" and the primary filter through which all subsequent projects and policies are evaluated. Without this vision, organizations often succumb to the **single most common reason for project failure**: starting with a technology ("we need to use AI") rather than a well-defined institutional problem ("reduce summer melt by 3%").

2. **Governance Before Technology:** The "governance-first" approach is the core recommendation emerging from analysis of successes and failures. The sources stress that the **most profound risk is not technological obsolescence, but ethical failure**. Establishing foundational principles and forming a **cross-functional AI Steering Committee** is the first and most critical action in the foundational phase of implementation (Phase 1: Crawl).

3. **Data Dependency:** While **data quality** is essential and data is often considered the **only remaining asset** in AI, the sources caution that data management initiatives (Pillar 2) will fail without a strong foundation in **Governance** (Pillar 5). **Compliance and access control policies** must be defined early in the lifecycle.

**Conclusion on Criticality:**

The most critical first step is therefore **establishing a clear, mission-aligned vision and ratifying the foundational AI Ethical Principles and Governance Structure**. This sets the non-negotiable standards for **Accountability** and **Fairness** before any specific project (MVAIP) is approved. If the risk of failure is deemed too high, managers are advised that an honest acknowledgement of the organization's lack of AI readiness, combined with a mid-term roadmap to improve capabilities, is better than rushing into a costly failure.
