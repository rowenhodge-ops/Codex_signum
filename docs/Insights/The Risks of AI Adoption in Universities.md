The sources consistently classify the risks associated with Artificial Intelligence (AI) adoption into two major, interconnected categories that impact universities: **Data/Security Risks** and **Ethical/Reputational Risks**. These risks necessitate a "governance-first" approach to mitigate potential harm.

1. Data and Security Risks

This category encompasses vulnerabilities related to the physical protection, integrity, privacy, and unauthorized use of institutional data and systems, which are magnified by AI's reliance on large datasets.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Specific Risk|Context and Implication for Universities|Source(s)|
|**Data Leakage and Privacy Violations**|The core data risk stems from the use of generative AI (GenAI) models, particularly publicly available, third-party services (e.g., ChatGPT). These tools are designed to **capture the data fed into them**, which may be used for training future products or released into the public domain. The unauthorized input of **Tier 3 (Restricted) or Tier 4 (Highly Restricted) data** (such as PII, PHI, or intellectual property) into these systems is **strictly prohibited** by institutions to prevent the transmission of sensitive data outside of the university's control or outside of Australia, leading to privacy breaches and potential legal repercussions. The collection and processing of sensitive student data, including academic records and behavioral patterns, raise concerns about privacy and security, especially compliance with regulations like FERPA and GDPR.||
|**Cybersecurity and Adversarial Attacks**|AI itself creates new avenues for malicious actors. Risks include **data poisoning** (corrupting training data to embed systematic biases or backdoors), **model evasion** (crafting inputs to deceive a model), and **model inversion** (extracting sensitive training data from the model). Malicious actors can also use AI to orchestrate more sophisticated and believable **phishing campaigns** or **spam messages** that exploit human vulnerabilities. Universities must defend against AI-specific threats, requiring security controls that go beyond traditional IT measures.||
|**Data Quality and Integrity**|Data is considered the "asset" and the "bottleneck". If the training data is poor, insufficient, or corrupted, the resulting AI model will be ineffective or dangerous. Poor data quality leads to high error rates and can expose the system to adversarial attacks. Robust **data governance frameworks** are essential for ensuring the quality, integrity, and security of data used by AI tools.||

2. Ethical and Reputational Risks

These risks relate to the AI system's impact on fairness, institutional trust, academic integrity, and human rights, leading to legal challenges and public failure if unaddressed.

|   |   |   |
|---|---|---|
|Specific Risk|Context and Implication for Universities|Source(s)|
|**Algorithmic Bias and Discrimination**|AI models are not objective; they learn from the data they are given, which often contains **historical or societal biases**. If left unchecked, the AI will **reproduce and amplify these biases**, leading to discriminatory outcomes in critical areas such as admissions, predictive advising, or assessment. Examples include disproportionately downgrading students from disadvantaged schools or penalizing non-native English speakers. Preventing bias is a core ethical principle requiring proactive effort.||
|**Erosion of Academic Integrity and Degree Value**|The widespread, often **unchecked use of generative AI** by students threatens to undermine the credibility of educational outcomes. Academics warn this could **"devalue degrees until they were 'handed out like expensive lollies'"**. The capacity of AI to generate essays and code makes it difficult to detect misconduct, leading to a breakdown of trust between students and the institution and risking the **competency of graduates** in critical fields. Universities must rapidly reform assessment practices to validate foundational learning.||
|**Inaccuracy and Reputational Damage**|Generative AI is prone to **"hallucination,"** or fabricating factually incorrect information that sounds authoritative. Using AI outputs in reports, decisions, or communications without verification can lead to **public failures, legal action, and catastrophic loss of trust**. The failure of an algorithmic system due to error or bias can cause **psychological distress, widespread protest, and legal challenges**. Institutions must be able to defend decisions, which requires **Transparency and Explainability** in AI systems.||
|**Lack of Accountability and Oversight**|When AI causes harm, the **"responsibility gap"** refers to the lack of clear human accountability for the system's actions. Effective governance requires **meaningful human oversight** for all AI systems, especially those making high-stakes decisions. Without clear lines of authority, the university faces significant legal and financial liability. The principle of **Contestability**—allowing students/staff to appeal or challenge an AI's decision—is necessary to maintain fairness and accountability.||
